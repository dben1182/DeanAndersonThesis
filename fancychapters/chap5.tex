\section{Safe Flight Corridor Path Following}

It is desirable for us to combine both of the major contributions of this paper, namely a trajectory following controller for an eVTOL and a path planning algorithm based on RRT to create safe flight corridors. A combination of these two contributions is for an eVTOL aircraft to traverse a trajectory generated over an obstacle field by the RRT Safe Flight Corridor algorithm. This will test whether the aircraft is capable of following a more complex shape than the simple root trajectories described earlier.

A 2D obstacle field was generated using the RRT algorithm on the North-Down plane. The start position was the origin and the end position was $(2000.0, 1000.0)$. Nine cubical obstacles of width $100.0$ were placed on a $3 \times 3$ grid. The RRT algorithm generated a smoothed SFC path, which is shown in Fig. \ref{fig:SFC_PathFollowing}. A video following the aircraft through the trajectory is found at: \href{https://youtu.be/hqRK5J1TDWM}{https://youtu.be/hqRK5J1TDWM}.

\subsection{Controller Failure}

In section \label{sec:Landing}, it was discussed that if the desired force in the aircraft's north body direction was less than zero ($F^b_{n,des} < 0$) the controller will not be able to control to that thrust. This is to say that if the high level controller sets a negative thrust for the forward-oriented rotor to create, it will be unable to do this due to saturation, and the controller will fail to follow the trajectory. This usually means the aircraft keeps moving at the same velocity, even though it was supposed to slow down or stop. 

Unfortunately, such is the case with many solutions to the 2D RRT flight controller test. Any trajectory that includes a significant downward section tends to cause divergence from the path.
